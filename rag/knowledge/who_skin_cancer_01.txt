SOURCE: WHO
TOPIC: Skin Cancer Prevention and UV Protection
---
Ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun is the primary cause of skin cancer worldwide. WHO recommends the following sun protection measures to reduce skin cancer risk:

1. Seek shade, especially during midday hours (10 AM to 4 PM) when UV radiation is strongest.
2. Wear protective clothing such as long-sleeved shirts, pants, a wide-brimmed hat, and UV-blocking sunglasses.
3. Use a broad-spectrum sunscreen with SPF 30 or higher. Apply liberally and reapply every two hours when outdoors.
4. Avoid tanning beds and sunlamps — they emit UV radiation and increase skin cancer risk.
5. Protect children — sunburns in childhood significantly increase the risk of melanoma later in life.

WHO estimates that 2 to 3 million non-melanoma skin cancers and 132,000 melanoma skin cancers occur globally each year. While the incidence is lower in darker-skinned populations such as South Asians, skin cancer can and does occur in Bangladeshi patients, particularly on sun-exposed areas of the face, hands, and forearms.

In Bangladesh, outdoor workers, farmers, and fishermen face prolonged sun exposure. Wearing protective clothing and seeking shade are the most practical prevention strategies. Patients noticing any non-healing skin lesion, rapidly growing lump, or unusual colored growth should visit a qualified dermatologist at a district hospital immediately.

This app screens for common fungal, inflammatory, and parasitic skin conditions. It does not screen for skin cancer. Suspected skin cancer requires urgent specialist referral.
